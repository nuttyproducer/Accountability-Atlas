# Brand and Tone Guide

## Brand personality

Accountability Atlas should feel:

- calm;
- serious;
- human;
- credible;
- precise;
- accessible;
- legally careful;
- urgent without being chaotic;
- emotionally grounded without being sensationalist.

---

## Voice

Use language that could be read by:

- a grieving civilian;
- a human-rights lawyer;
- a journalist;
- a policy advisor;
- a security reviewer;
- a skeptical NGO worker;
- a volunteer developer.

The voice should remain dignified under pressure.

---

## Preferred wording

Use:

- civilian protection;
- humanitarian access;
- verified evidence;
- alleged atrocity crimes;
- documented harm;
- legal accountability;
- lawful civic pressure;
- source methodology;
- public record;
- country accountability;
- arms-transfer review;
- policy action;
- correction process;
- evidence level;
- verified public source.

---

## Avoid wording

Avoid:

- revenge;
- destroy;
- enemy;
- traitor lists;
- expose them all;
- hunt;
- target;
- weaponize;
- punish civilians;
- collective blame;
- “proof” when there is only an allegation;
- “guilty” before a legal judgment;
- graphic or sensationalist captions.

---

## Legal caution words

Use carefully:

- alleged;
- reported;
- documented by;
- according to;
- concluded by;
- the court ordered;
- the prosecutor alleges;
- investigation opened;
- warrant issued;
- provisional measures;
- final judgment;
- public record;
- verified by source.

---

## Emotional tone

The platform may be emotionally powerful, but should not be emotionally uncontrolled.

Good:

> Every civilian life matters. Evidence must be preserved. Governments must be held to their legal and humanitarian obligations.

Bad:

> The whole world must fight them by any means necessary.

---

## Visual tone

Recommended:

- clean layout;
- sober typography;
- strong whitespace;
- restrained color palette;
- accessible contrast;
- clear source labels;
- map/report/document visual language;
- human-centered illustrations or photography only when licensed and respectful.

Avoid:

- shock imagery;
- graphic thumbnails;
- militaristic aesthetics;
- revenge symbolism;
- dehumanizing cartoons;
- flags used aggressively;
- gore as engagement bait.

---

## Color direction

Suggested palette:

- deep navy / charcoal for seriousness;
- off-white for readability;
- muted red for urgent alerts only;
- olive or teal for humanitarian/action accents;
- neutral grey for source metadata;
- amber for pending verification.

Do not use red everywhere. If everything is urgent, nothing is readable.

---

## Content hierarchy

Each page should answer:

1. What is the issue?
2. What is the evidence?
3. Who says so?
4. What is the legal or humanitarian relevance?
5. What can a person lawfully do?
6. How can errors be corrected?

---

## Public trust rules

- Every factual claim needs a source.
- Strong legal claims need stronger sources.
- Distinguish facts, allegations, legal findings, NGO conclusions, and advocacy positions.
- Do not imply partnerships without permission.
- Do not hide methodology.
- Do not exaggerate numbers.
- Do not publish content because it is viral; publish because it is verified or clearly labeled.
