# Legal Language Policy

This policy protects the credibility and safety of Accountability Atlas.

---

## Purpose

The platform deals with genocide allegations, war crimes, crimes against humanity, humanitarian obstruction, arms transfers, and international legal processes. Legal language must be accurate and careful.

---

## Core rules

1. Do not state legal guilt before a final judgment.
2. Distinguish allegations, investigations, warrants, findings, orders, and judgments.
3. Attribute conclusions to the organization or court that made them.
4. Use primary legal sources where possible.
5. Avoid exaggeration.
6. Avoid slogans in evidence pages.
7. Keep advocacy pages separate from evidence pages.

---

## Genocide terminology

The platform may discuss genocide in three legally distinct ways:

### 1. Court proceedings

Example:

> The ICJ is hearing a case concerning alleged violations of the Genocide Convention.

### 2. NGO or expert conclusions

Example:

> [Organization] concluded in [date] that [conduct] amounts to genocide / acts of genocide, according to its report.

### 3. Advocacy position

Example:

> The platform supports lawful action to prevent and punish atrocity crimes, including alleged genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity.

Do not write:

> The court has proven genocide.

unless a competent court has issued a final judgment saying so.

---

## ICC language

Use:

- arrest warrant issued;
- charges/allegations;
- prosecutor alleges;
- pre-trial chamber found reasonable grounds;
- not a conviction;
- proceedings ongoing.

Do not use:

- convicted;
- proven criminal;
- guilty;
- sentenced;

unless legally true.

---

## ICJ language

Use:

- application filed;
- provisional measures ordered;
- proceedings ongoing;
- final judgment pending;
- obligations under the Genocide Convention;
- state responsibility.

Do not treat provisional measures as final genocide findings.

---

## War crimes and crimes against humanity

Use careful attribution:

> [Source] reported evidence consistent with possible war crimes.

> [Organization] concluded that [conduct] may amount to crimes against humanity.

> [Court/prosecutor] alleges that [person] bears criminal responsibility for [charges].

---

## Arms transfer language

Use:

- arms-transfer review;
- suspension of arms exports;
- end-use monitoring;
- risk of serious violations of international humanitarian law;
- export-control obligations;
- parliamentary oversight;
- public export data.

Avoid unsupported claims of direct causation unless evidence exists.

---

## Country accountability language

Good:

> Belgium voted [x], stated [y], funded [z], and has not yet taken [action], according to public records.

Bad:

> Belgium supports genocide.

Scorecards must show methodology and sources.

---

## Individual accountability

Do not target private individuals.

Allowed:

- public officials;
- named people in public legal proceedings;
- command responsibility where public legal sources support it;
- public corporate actors where legally relevant.

Not allowed:

- private addresses;
- family members;
- personal phone numbers;
- social media harassment targets;
- revenge lists.

---

## Action language

Use:

- contact your representative;
- ask your government to support;
- request parliamentary review;
- demand humanitarian access;
- support lawful accountability;
- ask for an arms-transfer suspension;
- share verified evidence.

Avoid:

- attack;
- punish;
- hunt;
- destroy;
- make them pay;
- flood them;
- harass;
- by any means necessary.

---

## Review requirement

Pages that contain legal conclusions, court status, sanctions, indictments, or named individuals should be reviewed by someone with legal or human-rights expertise before public launch.
