# Methodology

This document explains how Accountability Atlas handles sources, claims, verification, legal language, corrections, and publication standards.

---

## Methodology goals

The platform aims to be:

- useful;
- accurate;
- transparent;
- legally careful;
- safe for affected people;
- credible to journalists, lawyers, policymakers, NGOs, and contributors.

---

## Core rule

No serious factual or legal claim should appear publicly without a source and a clear confidence level.

---

## Claim categories

### 1. Basic factual claim

Example:

> The ICC announced arrest warrants on a specific date.

Required source:

- official court page;
- official press release;
- primary legal document;
- reputable reporting if primary source is unavailable.

### 2. Humanitarian condition claim

Example:

> Aid access is restricted in a given area.

Required source:

- UN OCHA;
- UNRWA;
- ICRC/IFRC;
- MSF;
- other major humanitarian organization;
- reputable reporting with named source.

### 3. Legal status claim

Example:

> A court has ordered provisional measures.

Required source:

- court order;
- court press release;
- legal filing;
- official court page.

### 4. NGO legal conclusion

Example:

> A named NGO concluded that conduct amounts to genocide or acts of genocide.

Required source:

- the NGO’s official report;
- precise wording from the report;
- date of publication.

### 5. Incident claim

Example:

> An attack occurred at a specific location on a specific date.

Required source:

- primary official source;
- UN/humanitarian source;
- multiple reputable reports;
- verified OSINT;
- public media with geolocation/chronolocation method.

### 6. Social media lead

Example:

> A video circulating online appears to show an incident.

Required label:

- unreviewed lead;
- not independently verified;
- do not present as confirmed.

---

## Source hierarchy

Highest reliability for legal/factual baseline:

1. Courts and legal bodies: ICJ, ICC, domestic court records
2. UN bodies: OCHA, OHCHR, UNRWA, UNICEF, UNHCR, UN Commission of Inquiry
3. ICRC/IFRC and major humanitarian organizations
4. Major human-rights NGOs: Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, B'Tselem, Adalah, Physicians for Human Rights Israel, etc.
5. Government records: parliamentary records, export-control data, official statements
6. Reputable journalism with editorial standards
7. OSINT investigations with transparent method
8. Social media leads, only as unverified inputs

## Legal language principles

1. Do not state guilt before a final judgment.
2. Distinguish allegations from findings.
3. Distinguish NGO conclusions from court judgments.
4. Distinguish provisional measures from final rulings.
5. Use exact wording where legal precision matters.
6. Do not simplify legal status for emotional impact.

See [`legal-language-policy.md`](legal-language-policy.md).

---

## Evidence publication checklist

Before publishing an evidence item:

- [ ] Does it have at least one source?
- [ ] Is the source type identified?
- [ ] Is the verification level clear?
- [ ] Is the date clear?
- [ ] Is the location clear enough but not unsafe?
- [ ] Are casualty numbers sourced and attributed?
- [ ] Is graphic content avoided or responsibly gated?
- [ ] Are private persons protected?
- [ ] Is the legal wording careful?
- [ ] Is there a correction path?

---

## Casualty and harm figures

Casualty figures should be attributed, not presented as floating facts.

Good:

> According to [source], at least X people were reported killed as of [date].

Bad:

> X people were killed.

If sources disagree, show the range or explain the discrepancy.

---

## Corrections

Corrections are not a weakness. They are part of credibility.

Every public page should eventually include a correction link.

Corrections may involve:

- wrong date;
- wrong location;
- wrong source attribution;
- outdated legal status;
- mistranslation;
- duplicated incident;
- unverifiable media;
- privacy concern;
- unsafe exposure.

See [`correction-policy.md`](correction-policy.md).

---

## Review roles

Important content should be reviewed by the right people:

- legal claims: legal/human-rights reviewer;
- humanitarian claims: humanitarian/source reviewer;
- OSINT claims: OSINT reviewer;
- public action templates: legal/policy reviewer;
- sensitive content: safety/moderation reviewer;
- translations: fluent human reviewer.

---

## What we will not publish

Do not publish:

- private addresses;
- private contact details;
- family information;
- revenge lists;
- unverified accusations against private individuals;
- graphic content for shock value;
- raw sensitive witness material;
- personal data scraped from social media;
- content that could expose a vulnerable source;
- instructions for violence, evasion, or harassment.

---

## Methodology transparency

Each major public section should link back to methodology, source policy, legal language policy, and correction policy.
