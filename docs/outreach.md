# Outreach Plan

## Purpose

Outreach should attract serious collaborators, advisors, and future partners without overclaiming, spamming, or creating reputational risk.

---

## Outreach principles

1. Be calm and credible.
2. Do not exaggerate the project status.
3. Do not claim partnerships before confirmation.
4. Ask for specific help, not vague support.
5. Respect people’s time.
6. Avoid emotional pressure.
7. Keep messages short.
8. Record outreach privately and responsibly.
9. Do not share private contacts publicly.
10. Follow up once, not repeatedly.

---

## Outreach audiences

### Technical contributors

- civic-tech developers;
- open-source contributors;
- frontend/backend developers;
- data engineers;
- security/privacy engineers;
- designers;
- accessibility testers.

Ask:

> Can you help build the first static MVP or review the architecture?

### Research and methodology contributors

- OSINT researchers;
- journalists;
- human-rights researchers;
- academics;
- data analysts;
- policy researchers.

Ask:

> Can you review the source methodology or help structure one country page?

### Legal/human-rights reviewers

- human-rights lawyers;
- international law students;
- legal clinics;
- legal academics;
- accountability researchers.

Ask:

> Can you review our legal language policy and tell us what would make it safer and more accurate?

### Communications collaborators

- copywriters;
- social media strategists;
- translators;
- motion designers;
- newsletter writers.

Ask:

> Can you help communicate the project in a calm, credible, evidence-based way?

### Organizations

- humanitarian organizations;
- human-rights organizations;
- civic-tech groups;
- diaspora groups;
- student associations;
- journalism initiatives.

Ask first for review or feedback, not partnership.

## Outreach stages

### Stage 1 — Quiet credibility

Target: 10–20 trusted people.

Goal:

- feedback;
- first contributors;
- safety warnings;
- methodology review.

Message:

> We are preparing an open-source civic-tech accountability MVP. Could you review the PRD/methodology and tell us what is missing or unsafe?

### Stage 2 — Founding contributors

Target: public call on LinkedIn, GitHub, relevant communities.

Goal:

- developers;
- designers;
- researchers;
- translators;
- issue contributors.

Message:

> We are looking for people who can contribute 2–4 hours per week for 4 weeks.

### Stage 3 — Advisory circle

Target: legal, security, OSINT, humanitarian, communications advisors.

Goal:

- periodic review;
- credibility;
- safety.

Message:

> Could you advise on one specific part of the project?

### Stage 4 — Organization contact

Only after a demo exists.

Goal:

- feedback;
- permission to list public information accurately;
- possible future partnership.

Message:

> We are not asking for endorsement. We want to represent your public work accurately and invite corrections.

## First public call for collaborators

```text
I’m starting an open-source civic-tech project called Accountability Atlas.

The goal is to build a calm, credible, evidence-based platform for atrocity documentation, humanitarian accountability, and lawful citizen action — starting with Gaza and the wider regional humanitarian crisis, with a structure that can later support other crises too.

This is not a rage platform and not a personal campaign. The aim is to build serious public infrastructure: verified sources, legal trackers, country accountability pages, policy dossiers, humanitarian organization directories, and action tools that help citizens contact decision-makers in a lawful and informed way.

I’m looking for founding collaborators who can help shape this properly from the start:

- developers
- UX/UI designers
- legal or human-rights reviewers
- OSINT/research people
- data/privacy/security people
- copywriters and communication people
- translators
- civic-tech contributors

The project will start open-source on GitHub with a full PRD, contribution guide, code of conduct, source methodology, and safety principles.

This needs calm minds, serious builders, and people who care about civilian protection, humanitarian access, accountability, and human dignity.

If you want to help build something useful instead of only watching from the sidelines, reach out.
```

## Direct message template

```text
Hi [Name],

I’m starting an open-source civic-tech project called Accountability Atlas. The goal is to build a calm, credible platform for verified evidence, legal/humanitarian tracking, and lawful citizen action around atrocity accountability, starting with Gaza/oPt.

I’m not asking for a big commitment. I’m looking for careful people who can review one part or contribute a small task.

Based on your background in [area], would you be open to reviewing [specific document/page] or giving feedback on what could make it safer and more credible?

Repo: [link]

Thank you,
[Name]
```

## Organization review request template

```text
Subject: Accuracy review request — public organization directory entry

Dear [Organization/Team],

My name is [Name], and I am coordinating Accountability Atlas, an open-source civic-tech project focused on verified public evidence, humanitarian accountability, and lawful citizen action.

We are preparing a public directory of humanitarian and human-rights organizations so users can find official information and support channels. We do not want to imply partnership or endorsement without permission.

We would like to list your organization using only public information and include a clear disclaimer that the entry is informational unless you confirm otherwise.

Would someone on your team be willing to review the draft entry for accuracy?

Thank you,
[Name]
[Project link]
```

## Tracking outreach

Keep private records of:

- date contacted;
- person/organization;
- role;
- contact channel;
- message sent;
- response;
- follow-up date;
- permission status;
- notes.

Do not commit private outreach spreadsheets to GitHub.
